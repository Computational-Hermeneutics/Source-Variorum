# Plato, _Phaedrus_ — Jowett (1892) · Fowler (1925) · Wright (1888)

A three-witness **Text**-mode collation of the same dialogue in three English
translations of the Greek. The point of the sample is that the witnesses are not
copies of one text but **independent translations**, so almost every sentence is
a *substitution*: the readings correspond but the wording differs. It is the
ideal case for Source Variorum's **word-level refinement** — only the words that
actually diverge between the renderings are tinted, while shared names and
phrases stay clean. With three witnesses the **version hotspots** layer also
becomes meaningful: it marks where *all three* translators diverge, not just
where any pair does.

## Witnesses

| Siglum | Translation | Date | Character | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **J** | Benjamin Jowett | 1892 | *Free* / literary — fluent Victorian English, periphrastic | Project Gutenberg (public domain) |
| **F** | Harold N. Fowler | 1925 | *Literal* — the Loeb Classical Library facing-page translation, closer to the Greek syntax | PerseusDL / Perseus (public domain) |
| **W** | J. Wright | 1888 | *Literal*, Victorian — "mainly from the text of Bekker" | Internet Archive (public domain) — **uncorrected OCR** |

Jowett and Fowler are **complete, proofed** dialogues, processed to comparable
plain prose with normalised speaker labels (`SOCRATES:` / `PHAEDRUS:`); each is
roughly 23,000 words.

> **Wright is an uncorrected OCR witness, included for illustration.** It was
> machine-read from a 1888 scan and only *patterned* de-noise was applied
> (running headers and page numbers stripped, end-of-line hyphenation rejoined,
> the "Phaedrus" spelling and standalone "1"→"I" fixed, restructured into
> speaker turns). **Residual scan errors remain** — most visibly left-margin
> letter-clipping, e.g. "the **ps** walls" (for "city walls"), "the **vise**
> man" (for "wise man"). These surface in the braid as *spurious variants* that
> reflect the scan, not the translation. It is here to demonstrate three-witness
> behaviour and how OCR noise reads in a collation, not as a citable edition.

## What to look for

- The opening exchange alone shows the contrast: Jowett's "My dear Phaedrus,
  whence come you, and whither are you going?" against Fowler's "Dear Phaedrus,
  whither away, and where do you come from?" — a transposition of the two
  questions plus lexical substitution.
- Jowett tends to expand and smooth; Fowler tends to compress and track the
  Greek. The braid makes the translator's hand visible as a pattern across the
  whole dialogue.
- Because these are translations, "addition" / "omission" loci usually mark
  where one translator supplied or dropped connective material, not where the
  underlying text differs.
