# Plato, _Phaedrus_ — Jowett (1892) vs Fowler (1925)

A two-witness **Text**-mode collation of the same dialogue in two English
translations of the Greek. The point of the sample is that the witnesses are not
copies of one text but **independent translations**, so almost every sentence is
a *substitution*: the readings correspond but the wording differs. It is the
ideal case for Source Variorum's **word-level refinement** — only the words that
actually diverge between the two renderings are tinted, while shared names and
phrases stay clean.

## Witnesses

| Siglum | Translation | Date | Character | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **J** | Benjamin Jowett | 1892 | *Free* / literary — fluent Victorian English, periphrastic | Project Gutenberg (public domain) |
| **F** | Harold N. Fowler | 1925 | *Literal* — the Loeb Classical Library facing-page translation, closer to the Greek syntax | PerseusDL / Perseus (public domain) |

Both files are the **complete dialogue**, processed to comparable plain prose
with normalised speaker labels (`SOCRATES:` / `PHAEDRUS:`). Each is roughly
13,000 words.

## What to look for

- The opening exchange alone shows the contrast: Jowett's "My dear Phaedrus,
  whence come you, and whither are you going?" against Fowler's "Dear Phaedrus,
  whither away, and where do you come from?" — a transposition of the two
  questions plus lexical substitution.
- Jowett tends to expand and smooth; Fowler tends to compress and track the
  Greek. The braid makes the translator's hand visible as a pattern across the
  whole dialogue.
- Because these are translations, "addition" / "omission" loci usually mark
  where one translator supplied or dropped connective material, not where the
  underlying text differs.
