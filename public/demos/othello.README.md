# _Othello_ — Globe (1866) vs Craig / Oxford (1914)

A two-witness **Text**-mode collation of Shakespeare's *Othello* in two
public-domain critical editions printed about fifty years apart. Unlike the
*Phaedrus* sample (two translations), here both witnesses descend from the same
early texts (the 1622 Quarto and 1623 First Folio), so the differences are
**editorial**: how two editors normalised spelling, repunctuated, relineated
verse, and placed stage directions and act/scene headings.

## Witnesses

The sample is a **four-witness** project — open any two in the panels and pick
the others from the Sources sidebar. Three are modernised editions (editorial
differences: spelling, punctuation, lineation — "bombast"/"bumbast",
"Off-capp'd"/"Off--capp'd"); the **First Folio (1623)** is the original-spelling
witness ("Neuer tell me", "vnkindly", "abhorre", "heauie"), so collating it
against any modern edition surfaces deep textual and orthographic variation —
e.g. the Folio's opening lacks the "Tush!" the modern editions print. The 1622
Quarto in original spelling would be the natural fifth witness.

Both files are the complete play (~27,500 words each), scraped and normalised to
a common speaker-name and verse-line shape so that the collation surfaces genuine
editorial divergence rather than transcription noise.

## What to look for

- **Punctuation and capitalisation**: the two editions repeatedly differ in comma
  placement, colons vs semicolons, and capitalised line-initial words.
- **Lineation**: short shared lines are split differently — e.g. "Abhor me." is set
  as its own line in Craig but run on in Globe — producing addition/omission loci
  at line boundaries.
- **Stage directions and headings**: entrances, exits, and act/scene headers are
  worded and placed differently, a classic site of editorial intervention.

A genuine modern critical edition (e.g. Arden, Oxford 2006) would be the richest
witness but is in copyright and cannot ship publicly; these two public-domain
editions still yield ~2,000 real editorial variants over ~1,080 matching
sentences.
